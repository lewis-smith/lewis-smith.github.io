how do we get better?

We sat down in a trendy art cafe in Chiang Mai. We wanted to work out what to do next with the app. We had a few unfinished features and a few unanswered questions. In short we needed some direction.

We started writing on cards all the things we wanted to do in all areas of the app, features, marketing, everything. As we got them down it became clear that there was a kind of hierarchy of goals. And we sorted the cards into the appropriate category.

Here’s the hierarchy that we saw:

Although, they all feed into each other, it’s possible to address things at the higher levels specifically. For example, getting more reviews could come from thinking more carefully about how and when we ask for reviews. And more downloads could come from improved screenshots or app store wording.

This was kind of a revelation for me, as a developer it’s so easy to get tied into the ground level view where you want to add features. The reviews thing in particular, we have a bit of code that prompts the user after a number of times of using the app and we thought about the wording and how many uses was ok before asking. But we didn’t think much beyond that. Might things like time of day make a difference? The more we thought about it the more we thought it would.

We also realised there was a lot more analytics we could be collecting to help us understand our customers better and make more informed decisions about which features to include.

So we’ve decided to park the new features and focus purely on the science of getting more review and more downloads for a little bit. As well as collecting more data to help us decide how to approach increasing engagement.

Here’s the plan:

  • Refine which screens on the app will ask a user for a review. Never ask just when the app opens
  • Add reporting to know how many times we ask for a review and how many times people say yes, no and later
  • A/B test the time of day to ask for a review. I have a theory that for our app asking in the afternoon will give better results than in the morning, but I want to test that
  • Add events for data collection and add goals in analytics
  • Collect data about which model our users are using. For whatever reason analytics doesn’t gather this by default. This is particularly important as iOS 7 approaches. As some devices will not be able to upgrade

The Beginning

It all started with trying to fit into my wedding suit – as all good app development stories do. A healthy intake of beers and burgers had given me a few too many extra inches around the belly and those inches needed to come off. Diets aren’t really my thing but Tim Ferriss’ Four Hour Body appealed to the geek in me because it’s closer to science than most. He’s dug into the chemistry, there’s a definite ‘right and wrong’ way of doing it (the fewer decisions required, the better), and he measures everything – in spreadsheets, no less. If ever there was a man-friendly diet, this is it.

One of 4HB’s commandments is that you consistently measure your arms, hips, waist and legs in order to calculate your Total Body Measurements, and then track this figure along with your body fat percentage. But the spreadsheet just didn’t cut it.

I’d been looking to get into iPhone development for a while so I decided to roll my own body measurements tracking app as a learning project. As it was just for me it didn’t need to be too polished, but being a big fan of Apple products I also wanted to include the native controls and gestures to make it feel as much like a proper iPhone app as possible.

The first version was pretty basic, but way better than a spreadsheet. After using it for a while it occurred to me that other 4HBers might also want an easier way of tracking their measurements. So, without polishing or refining, I put it on the App Store to see what happened. And people downloaded it. Not in their thousands, but enough to make me think there was a market I could tap into to make some money. So there it was: No monetization strategy, no marketing plan, no slick design or well thought out UI. My first app was quick and dirty, but it’s evolved into something I’m really proud of. And it helped me get into that wedding suit.
